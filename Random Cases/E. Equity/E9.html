Lim Chin San Contractors Pte Ltd v Shiok Kim Seng (trading as IKO Precision Toolings)

Lim Hock Kim v Sim Seng Quee
[1979-1980] SLR(R) 494; [1980] SGHC 11

Case Number:District Court Appeal No 46 of 1979
Decision Date:02 April 1980
Tribunal/Court:High Court
Coram: Choor Singh J
Counsel Name(s): N Ganesan (Ganesan Carlose & Partners) for the appellant; Cheng Tim Pin (Yap & Yap) for the respondent.
Parties: Lim Hock Kim — Sim Seng Quee

Equity – Estoppel – Tenant built houses on land – Whether landlord estopped from recovering possession of land – Whether tenancy coupled with an equity – Whether landlord consented to, induced or encouraged tenant to construct houses on land

Landlord and Tenant – Termination of leases – Tenant built houses on land – Landlord claiming vacant possession of land – Whether tenancy coupled with equity – Whether landlord estopped from recovering possession of land

Facts

The defendant was let onto the plaintiff’s vacant land. He constructed five small wooden houses on the land and sold four of the houses. He retained possession of one house together with the land on which it stood and which was the subject matter of the plaintiff’s subsequent claim for vacant possession.

The defendant alleged that the plaintiff’s predecessor in title had expressly or impliedly consented to the building of the houses, and that he was induced to believe he could build on and occupy the land for as long as he wished so long as the monthly ground rent was paid. He thus claimed that his tenancy was coupled with an equity and the plaintiff was estopped from recovering possession of the land in question.

The Senior District Judge allowed the plaintiff’s claim for vacant possession and the defendant appealed.

Held, dismissing the appeal:

(1)    For equity to intervene there must be evidence of some inducement or encouragement by the landlord or some expectation held out by him to the tenant on the strength of which the tenant expended money on the land: at [7] and [8].

(2)    There was no evidence of any express or implied consent given for the erection of the houses nor was there any evidence of inducement or encouragement by the landlord. The defendant was not entitled to any equitable relief: at [9] and [10].

Case(s) referred to

Khew Ah Bah v Hong Ah Mye [1971–1973] SLR(R) 107; [1969–1971] SLR 494 (refd)

Ramsden v Dyson (1866) LR 1 HL 129 (refd)

Wee Kang Whye v Lee Woon Tong [1971–1973] SLR(R) 826; [1972–1974] SLR 526 (refd)

2 April 1980

Choor Singh J:

1       This was an appeal from the judgment of the learned Senior District Judge, Mr Michael Khoo Kah Lip, who allowed the plaintiff’s claim for vacant possession of the land occupied by the defendant.

2       The facts are these. The defendant was let into the land which was then vacant in 1957 as a ground tenant on a month-to-month ground rent of $91.50 per month. A notice to quit dated 7 April 1975 was served on 9 April 1975 purportedly determining the tenancy from 31 May 1975. The provisions of the Control of Rent Act do not apply to the land in question.

3       At some time after the defendant was let into the land he constructed five small wooden houses on the land. He sold four of them and granted the purchasers 30-year leases of the land on which the houses stood. In those leases the defendant described himself as “owner” of the land. The defendant has retained possession of one of the five houses together with the land on which it stands and that is the subject matter of this case.

4       It was alleged by the defendant that the five wooden houses were built between 1959 and 1960 and that the plaintiff’s predecessor-in-title was fully aware that the defendant was proceeding to build them and “expressly and/or impliedly consented to the same”. The plaintiff’s predecessor-in-title was his father, Sim Khong. It was further alleged that the defendant was induced to believe that he “could build on the said land and to occupy the said land for as long as the defendant wished so long as monthly ground rent was paid”.

5       In short, the defendant claimed that his tenancy was coupled with an equity and that the plaintiff was estopped from recovering possession of the land in question.

6       The defendant relied on the decisions in Ramsden v Dyson (1866) LR 1 HL 129, Khew Ah Bah v Hong Ah Mye [1971–1973] SLR(R) 107 and Wee Kang Whye v Lee Woon Tong [1971–1973] SLR(R) 826. These cases show that if a landlord allows his tenant to expend money on the land under an expectation created or encouraged by the landlord that he will be able to remain there, the tenant has a tenancy coupled with an equity. The landlord is thus precluded from recovering possession of his land by merely terminating his tenancy; he must also satisfy the equity.

7       The burden of proof was on the defendant. The learned Senior District Judge clearly rejected the contention of the defendant that Sim Khong stood idly by whilst the defendant erected the houses on his land. Having heard all the evidence and the submissions made, he made the following finding:

Having regard to all the facts in this case, I am not satisfied that there is evidence that the landlord had encouraged or induced the defendant to build the houses, or that there was any expectation held out by him to the defendant on the strength of which the defendant expended money on the land. No evidence has been forthcoming to show conduct on the part of Sim Khong which induced the defendant to build the houses in the belief that rights in perpetuity would thereby be acquired. No equity has been shown to arise from the expenditure of money by the defendant …

8       In Wee Kang Whye’s case ([6] supra) at [15], I stated:

… For equity to intervene there must be evidence of some inducement or encouragement by the landlord or some expectation held out by him to the tenant on the strength of which the tenant expended money on the land …

9       There was no such evidence in this case. There was no evidence of any express or implied consent given for the erection of the houses nor was there any evidence of inducement or encouragement by the landlord. There were no grounds at all for equity to intervene on behalf of the defendant.

10     In my judgment, the defendant was not entitled to any equitable relief and the learned Senior District Judge came to a correct decision in allowing the plaintiff’s claim for vacant possession of the land occupied by the defendant.
