Lim Chin San Contractors Pte Ltd v Shiok Kim Seng (trading as IKO Precision Toolings)

Lim Sui Hong Katherine v Lim Joo Hien Anthony
[1998] 3 SLR(R) 612; [1998] SGCA 63

Case Number:Civil Appeal No 267 of 1997
Decision Date:09 October 1998
Tribunal/Court:Court of Appeal
Coram: Yong Pung How CJ; M Karthigesu JA; L P Thean JA
Counsel Name(s): Pala Krishnan and P B Balagopal (Palakrishnan & Pnrs) for the appellant.Han Kee Fong (Tan Rajah & Cheah) for the respondent.
Parties: Lim Sui Hong Katherine — Lim Joo Hien Anthony

Equity – Maxims – Whether there was compromise agreement which supervened rights under previous agreement – Whether two inconsistent claims being put forward by party seeking relief – Principles for application of maxim “he who seeks equity must do equity”

Facts

The plaintiff, Anthony Lim, and the defendant, Katherine Lim, are the lawful children of David Lim Eng Guan, who died testate, having made his last will and testament bequeathing his family home to the plaintiff and defendant in equal shares in the event his wife predeceased him, which she did.

The defendant, as sole executrix of David Lim’s estate, did not regard the family home as part of his estate because she claimed that there was an equitable disposition of the property, inter vivos, to her. The plaintiff disputed her claim and both parties subsequently entered into an oral agreement whereby the plaintiff agreed to renounce his right to a half share in the property in return for the defendant paying him £100,000. The defendant then sent the plaintiff two bank drafts for £12,500 each in part fulfilment of this oral agreement.

The plaintiff later changed his mind and having returned the two bank drafts, claimed his interest in the property and commenced proceedings against the defendant to compel her to administer the estate in accordance with the will.

The learned judge found that the equitable disposition to the defendant was valid. He also found that the defendant should be bound by the oral agreement she had with the plaintiff which supervened the plaintiff’s rights under the will and the defendant’s rights under the agreement she had with her late father. Since a person who seeks equity must also do equity, she would have to honour the agreement by paying the plaintiff £100,000 with interest.

The defendant appealed against the order.

Held, allowing the appeal:

(1)    Whilst there was an agreement between the plaintiff and the defendant, it was not a compromise agreement which supervened both the plaintiff’s rights under the will and the defendant’s rights under the agreement she had with her late father. There was no correlation between the agreement the defendant had with her late father and the agreement she had with the plaintiff. The defendant set up her agreement with her late father firstly in defence of the plaintiff’s claim for the administration of the estate in accordance with the terms of his will and secondly by her counterclaim to assert her entitlement in equity to the property. Her agreement with the plaintiff was put up as an alternative to her claim to equitable entitlement to the property. It is a fundamental rule of pleading that a claim may be pleaded in the alternative. She did not “approbate and reprobate”: at [11] and [12].

(2)    Since it was the plaintiff who resiled from his oral agreement by returning the two bank drafts for which the defendant had sent him in part fulfilment of the oral agreement and commenced the action, the oral agreement was no longer subsisting: at [13].

(3)    The judge erroneously invoked the equitable maxim of “he who seeks equity must do equity”. This maxim means that in granting relief in equity, the court may require a person who comes to enforce an equitable claim to submit to any directions which the known principles of a court of equity may make it proper to give. However this does not mean that the court can impose arbitrary conditions on the claimant merely because he stands in that position. He must do justice as to the matters in respect of which the assistance of equity is asked: at [14] and [15].

(4)    As a general rule, a plaintiff in equity should never be compelled to give to the defendant anything but what a defendant might, as a plaintiff, enforce, provided a cause of suit arose. The plaintiff in this case could not have enforced the oral agreement against the defendant in the reverse case: at [15].

Case(s) referred to

Hanson v Keating (1884) 4 Hare 1; 67 ER 537 (refd)

9 October 1998

M Karthigesu JA (delivering the grounds of decision of the court:

1       The appellant (“Katherine”) and the respondent (“Anthony”) are the lawful children of David Lim Eng Guan (“David Lim”), deceased. David Lim died on 4 February 1995. He died testate having made his last will and testament on 25 March 1980 by which he bequeathed his family home, 59A Paterson Road (“the property”) to Katherine and Anthony in equal shares in the event his wife Anna Lim predeceased him. Anna Lim did predecease him.

2       Katherine, now as the sole executrix named in David Lim’s will, was granted probate of it on 5 June 1995. She extracted the grant of probate on 13 July 1995 but did not proceed to administer the estate as in her view the property did not form part of the estate of David Lim, deceased, because there was an equitable disposition of the property, inter vivos, to her. Her late father had agreed to transfer the property to her in consideration of the financial support Katherine had given him for many years and still continued to give but before he could do this he had died. She therefore claimed an equitable right to the whole of the property.

3       Anthony, who had been living in the United Kingdom for many years and who had now taken up British Nationality, however, was not of the same view. He came to Singapore to discuss his entitlement to his late father’s estate with Katherine. It was not disputed by either party that an agreement was reached whereby Anthony agreed to renounce his right to a half share in the property in return for Katherine paying him £100,000 sterling. It was also not in dispute that Katherine, in honour of this oral agreement, sent to Anthony, after he had returned to the United Kingdom, two bank drafts for £12,500 each, one made out to Anthony and the other made out to Anthony’s wife as requested by him, in part fulfilment of this oral agreement.

4       However, Anthony changed his mind and, having first returned the two drafts referred to above, claimed his interest in the property and commenced proceedings against Katherine to compel her to administer the estate of David Lim, deceased, in accordance with his will. Katherine, as already indicated in [2] and [3] above, pleaded the equitable disposition of the property, inter vivos, to her (giving full particulars) and in the alternative the oral agreement with Anthony (also giving full particulars) and counterclaimed for declarations that the equitable disposition of the property, inter vivos, to her was valid and that Anthony had no interest in the property; alternatively, she claimed that in the event Anthony succeeded in his claim, a declaration that the oral agreement she had made with Anthony was valid and binding and for an order for specific performance of the agreement by Anthony.

5       The action commenced by Anthony was tried by Justice G P Selvam. The learned judge found that on the evidence Katherine was in equity entitled to the property for the reason that her late father, David Lim, was under a contractual obligation to transfer it to her. With this finding we are in total agreement. Anthony has not appealed against it.

6       However, the learned judge went on to say that as Katherine had asked the court for an equitable remedy, she had to accept that the court could also make such orders as it saw fit to ensure that equity would be done. On this basis he found that apart from the fact that Katherine was entitled in equity to the property in view of the agreement she had with her late father, Katherine should also be bound by the subsequent oral agreement that she had made with Anthony after their father’s death. The learned judge also found that this so called compromise agreement as articulated by Katherine herself supervened Anthony’s rights under the will and Katherine’s rights under the agreement she had with her late father. Basing his decision on the maxim that he who seeks equity must do equity, G P Selvam J decided that Katherine was bound by the agreement she had made with Anthony. Katherine would have to honour it as she had affirmed it all the time. She could not approbate and reprobate.

7       Accordingly, the learned judge made an order that Katherine was at liberty to transfer the property to herself acting in her capacity as the sole executrix of the estate of David Lim either by way of assent or otherwise provided she paid Anthony the sum of £100,000 sterling together with interest at the rate of 6% pa from the date of the so-called compromise agreement to the date of judgment. On the question of costs, the learned judge said: “In view of the inconsistent position taken by her (Katherine) I further decided that each party must bear its own costs.”

8       Katherine appealed against this order. We allowed her appeal, set aside the order made by the learned judge and in its place ordered that Katherine was entitled to (a) a declaration that the agreement she had with her late father to transfer the property to her was valid and binding; (b) a declaration that Anthony had no interest in the property; and (c) an order for the costs of the hearing before G P Selvam J as well as the costs of the appeal. We now give our reasons.

9       We have already expressed our agreement with the learned judge’s finding that Katherine was entitled in equity to the property (see [5] above). This finding, in our view, would have been sufficient to dispose of Anthony’s claim made against Katherine in her capacity as the sole executrix of the estate of David Lim, deceased, for a distribution to him of his share in the estate of David Lim (ie the property) in accordance with the will.

10     But, having regard no doubt to Katherine’s counterclaim and the declarations she sought the learned judge quite properly was impelled to consider the oral agreement between Katherine and Anthony (see [3] above). He stated his finding, without giving reasons, as follows (Lim Joo Hien Anthony v Katherine Lim Sui Hong [1998] SGHC 61):

I found as a matter of fact that there was a compromise as articulated by her [Katherine] in the form of the Anthony Lim agreement which supervened the plaintiff’s [Anthony’s] rights under the will and the defendant’s [Katherine’s] right under the ‘David Lim agreement’.

The “David Lim agreement” was referred to by the learned judge in his grounds of judgment as follows:

In her pleadings the defendant took an incongruous position. In a synopsis, the position taken by her was that in consideration of her providing financial maintenance and support to David Lim, deceased he had agreed to transfer the property to her. She fulfilled her part of the bargain and therefore in equity she was the owner of the flat. To strengthen her case she said that in January 1995 arrangements had been made to make a formal transfer of the Paterson Road flat [the property] to her. However, it could not be achieved because of the intervention of Chinese New Year holidays. Before the transfer of the property could be effected the father passed away on 4 February 1995. She, therefore, in equity, was entitled to an entire interest in the Paterson Road flat and therefore the flat was not part of the estate of David Lim.

What the learned judge termed as “the Anthony Lim agreement” was referred to thus in his grounds of judgment:

… After he [Anthony] was informed of the demise of David Lim deceased, according to the defendant [ie Katherine], he agreed to renounce his interest in the Paterson Road flat. Shortly hereafter he came to Singapore and made a volte face and claimed his half interest in the estate [ie the property] in accordance with the will. Then, according to the defendant, he wavered again and agreed not to make any claim on the immovable property if the defendant would give him a sum of £100,000 to settle his personal debts. The plaintiff [Anthony]) the [sic] agreed to finally renounce his claim in exchange for a half-share in the Paterson Road flat under the will. The defendant agreed. Pursuant to this agreement she went ahead and purchased two bank drafts of £12,500 and gave them to him. He changed his mind again and claimed his full interest under the will.

11     We were unable to agree with the learned judge that there was a compromise agreement which supervened both Anthony’s rights under the will and Katherine’s rights under the agreement she had with her late father. There was an agreement but to say that it compromised the agreement Katherine had with her late father and her late father’s will went beyond the pleadings and the evidence given at the trial. Anthony, by his reply and defence to counterclaim, not only joined issue with Katherine’s defence and counterclaim but also denied that there was any such agreement as contended by Katherine between her and her late father to transfer the property to her in consideration for all the financial maintenance and support she had given and continued to give her late parents since 1983. In fact the learned judge found that there was such an agreement (see [5] above). Plainly and simply the agreement Katherine had with Anthony was that Anthony would renounce his right to a half share under the will in consideration of which Katherine would pay Anthony £100,000 sterling. This is how, in essence, Katherine pleaded the agreement in her defence and counterclaim and this was fully supported by our reading of the evidence.

12     In our view there was no correlation between the agreement Katherine had with her late father and the agreement she had with Anthony. It was clear to us from the pleadings that Katherine set up her agreement with her late father firstly in defence of Anthony’s claim for the administration of David Lim’s estate in accordance with the terms of his will and secondly by her counterclaim to assert her entitlement in equity to the property. Her agreement with Anthony was put up as an alternative to her claim to the equitable entitlement to the property. The learned judge seems to have overlooked a fundamental rule of pleading that a claim may be pleaded in the alternative. It is worth quoting the relevant paragraph of the amended defence and counterclaim. It reads:

11    Alternatively, the defendant (Katherine) also counterclaims against the plaintiff (Anthony) in the manner as set out in para 12–19 hereafter (ie her agreement with Anthony) which, for avoidance of doubt, is only pleaded should the plaintiff succeed in his allegation, at para 4 of the statement of claim, that the testator was, at the date of his death, possessed of the immovable property (ie the property).

Nothing could be plainer. With respect the learned judge erred in taking the view that Katherine was putting forward two inconsistent claims at one and the same time, to use the learned judge’s words, “She could not approbate and reprobate.”

13     So the learned judge having found that there was an oral agreement between Katherine and Anthony that Anthony would renounce his right to a half share in the property in consideration of Katherine paying him £100,000 sterling and being of the view (erroneously as we have said earlier) that this agreement supervened both Anthony’s rights under the will and Katherine’s rights under the agreement she had with her father proceeded as though the oral agreement between Katherine and Anthony was still subsisting. Again the learned judge seems to have overlooked the fact that it was Anthony and not Katherine who had resiled from this oral agreement. It will be remembered that Anthony returned the two drafts for £12,500 which Katherine had sent to Anthony in part fulfilment of the oral agreement and commenced the action in this appeal. In our view the oral agreement was no longer subsisting.

14     With respect to the learned judge the combined effect of the two errors we have discussed in [12] and [13] above led him erroneously to invoke the equitable maxim of “he who seeks equity must do equity” and thus to do a grave injustice to Katherine by enforcing on her an agreement which the other party, Anthony, had resiled from.

15     Again, with respect, the learned judge misapplied the maxim, if it was applicable at all. An explanation of this maxim is given in Halsbury’s Laws of England vol 16 (Butterworths, 4th Ed, reissue) paras 749 and 750. Basically, it means that in granting relief in equity, the court may require a man who comes to enforce an equitable claim to submit to any directions which the known principles of a court of equity may make it proper to give. However, this does not mean that the court can impose arbitrary conditions on the claimant merely because he stands in that position. He must do justice as to the matters in respect of which the assistance of equity is asked. As explained by Sir James Wigram VC in Hanson v Keating (1884) 4 Hare 1 at 6; 67 ER 537 a party does not by becoming a plaintiff in equity, give up any of his rights or submit those rights to the arbitrary disposition of the court. In fact as a general rule, the plaintiff should never be compelled to give to the defendant anything but what a defendant might, as a plaintiff, enforce, provided a cause of suit arose. In our opinion on the facts of this case Anthony could not have enforced the oral agreement against Katherine in the reverse case.

16     It was for these reasons that we allowed this appeal. We had no hesitation in setting aside the orders made by the learned judge and making the orders we did.
