Lim Chin San Contractors Pte Ltd v Shiok Kim Seng (trading as IKO Precision Toolings)

Lee Suat Hong v Teo Lye
[1987] SLR(R) 70; [1987] SGCA 4

Case Number:Civil Appeal No 49 of 1986
Decision Date:25 February 1987
Tribunal/Court:Court of Appeal
Coram: Wee Chong Jin CJ; A P Rajah J; Chan Sek Keong JC
Counsel Name(s): Ng Liang Poh (Ng Liang Poh & Co) for the appellant; Cheong Yuen Hee and Mabel Tan (Cheong Yeo & Partners) for the respondent.
Parties: Lee Suat Hong — Teo Lye

Civil Procedure – Appeals – Finding of fact by trial judge – Principles on which appellate court may interfere – Whether distinction drawn between trial judge’s finding of fact and drawing of inference

Equity – Estoppel – Promissory estoppel – Estoppel by acquiescence – Owner of land consented to respondent building house on land – Respondent expended money in building house – Whether extent of respondent’s equity permanently protected her occupation of land

Equity – Estoppel – Promissory estoppel – Proper approach for court to take – Whether representation made to respondent – Extent of equity established by estoppel – Manner in which equity could be best satisfied

Facts

The appellant had sued the respondent for possession of the premises at 38A Lorong Puntong (“38A”), together with arrears of rent and mesne profits from 1 February 1983 and costs. The appellant alleged that the respondent had been in possession of 38A as his tenant and that the tenancy had been terminated by a notice to quit which expired on 31 January 1983.

The respondent claimed an equitable estoppel in her favour, and contended that she was entitled to stay at 38A, where she and her family had resided since about 1956, permanently. The respondent alleged that the appellant’s predecessor-in-title had represented to the respondent and her family that if they gave up possession of a plot of land at Mukim 15, the respondent and her family could erect their own house on 38A and stay on 38A for as long as they deemed fit.

At the first instance, the trial judge found that the respondent did not actually know the terms of occupation of 38A, or the terms under which her husband was allowed to build the house at 38A. The trial judge thus found that no expectation that the respondent could stay at 38A permanently had been created.

The respondent successfully appealed to the High Court. The High Court found that an equitable estoppel had been established, and held that this estoppel protected the respondent and her family’s occupation of 38A permanently.

The appellant appealed.

Held, allowing the appeal:

(1)    Where the defendant raises equitable estoppel to restrict the plaintiff from exercising his legal rights as landowner, the proper approach is to inquire first, whether any equitable estoppel exists, and if so, the extent of the equity established by the estoppel, before considering how best that equity may be satisfied: at [13].

(2)    No expectation had been created that the respondent would be allowed to stay at 38A permanently. The court would not disturb the trial judge’s finding of fact in this regard. This was because the finding of fact in this case related to a finding of a specific fact (ie whether the representation as alleged by the respondent had been made) and not to the drawing of an inference. While an appellate court would be more willing to form an independent opinion upon the proper inference to be drawn, it would be extremely reluctant to reject the trial judge’s finding in this case, as the finding related to a primary fact deposed to by witnesses and had been based on the credibility and bearing of the witnesses: at [18] and [19].

(3)    However, an equitable estoppel or estoppel by acquiescence arose in the respondent’s favour as the respondent had expended money in building her house on 38A with the then owner’s consent. This equity had to be satisfied before the appellant could recover possession: at [20].

(4)    While the extent of the respondent’s equity did not protect her occupation of 38A permanently, it protected her from eviction without compensation. The respondent’s equity would thus be satisfied by compensating the respondent for the costs of erecting the house: at [21] and [22].

Case(s) referred to

Goh Leng Kang v Teng Swee Lin [1974–1976] SLR(R) 124 (refd)

25 February 1987

Wee Chong Jin CJ (delivering the judgment of the court):

1       This appeal arises from a claim by the plaintiff/appellant against the defendant/respondent for possession of premises known as 38A, Lorong Puntong, Singapore, together with arrears of rent and mesne profits from 1 February 1983 and costs.

2       The plaintiff alleged that the defendant had been in possession of the premises as the plaintiff’s tenant and that the tenancy had been duly determined by notice to quit expiring on 31 January 1983.

3       The defendant claimed to be entitled to continued possession of the premises on the ground that the plaintiff was estopped from denying the defendant’s right to remain in occupation of the premises for as long as the defendant and her family deemed fit.

4       The defence of estoppel is based on the allegation that the plaintiff’s predecessor-in-title of the present premises (“38A”) had represented to the defendant and her family that if they were to give up possession of Lot 234, Mukim 15, of which the defendant claimed to have been protected tenants under the Control of Rent Act, the defendant and her family would be allowed to erect their own house on the present premises and to remain in occupation for as long as they deem fit.

5       The defendant further alleged that relying on this representation, the defendant and her family gave up possession of Lot 234, Mukim 15, moved to 38A, erected their own house on it and incurred expenses in reclaiming part of the premises which was then swamp land.

6       In substance therefore the defendant’s contention was that an equitable estoppel had been established, creating an equity in her favour which could only be satisfied by permitting her to stay at 38A permanently. The defendant also argued that alternatively, the equity could be satisfied if the plaintiff were to pay her $80,000, being the costs of resettling her in a four-room HDB flat.

7       The plaintiff denied that any expectation that the defendant could stay at 38A permanently, had been created. However, the plaintiff did not dispute that the defendant, having expended money in building the house at 38A with the then owner’s consent, did possess an equity which had to be satisfied before the plaintiff could obtain possession. The plaintiff contended that this equity would be satisfied if the defendant were compensated for the replacement cost of the house. This replacement cost was valued at $24,207 less 25% depreciation for age, by a valuer whose appointment had been agreed upon by both parties. The plaintiff was prepared to pay the defendant the full replacement value of $24,207 without any deduction for depreciation.

8       The trial judge found on the evidence before him that the defendant did not actually know the terms of the occupation of 38A. Neither did the defendant know the conditions under which her husband was allowed to build the house at 38A. The trial judge was also of the view that the defendant was not a protected tenant of Lot 234 as pleaded. In the circumstances the trial judge found that no expectation that the defendant could stay at 38A permanently had been created.

9       The trial judge then ordered the plaintiff to pay the defendant $24,207, being the replacement cost of the defendant’s house at 38A before the plaintiff could recover possession. The trial judge rejected the defendant’s claim for $80,000 compensation in satisfaction of her equity, so that she could move to a four-room HDB flat. In his view the compensation of $24,207 was fair and reasonable.

10     The defendant appealed to the High Court and succeeded in her appeal. The High Court in allowing the appeal held that:

(a)     the trial judge had erred in concluding that no expectation was created by the plaintiff’s predecessor-in-title that the defendant and her family could reside at 38A permanently;

(b)     the defendant had proved on the balance of probabilities that the circumstances of the case established the equitable estoppel alleged by her; and

(c)     that the equitable estoppel protected the defendant and her family’s occupation of the premises permanently.

11     The court further expressed the view that even if the plaintiff were to be entitled to possession, the extent of the defendant’s equity which the plaintiff would have to satisfy would be the cost of resettling the defendant and not just the replacement cost of the house built. The court accepted the defendant’s submission that the cost of resettling the defendant would be $80,000.

12     The plaintiff, being dissatisfied with the High Court’s decision, appealed and we allowed the appeal and restored the decision of the trial judge.

13     In our view, in cases such as this where the defendant raises equitable estoppel to restrict the plaintiff from exercising his legal rights as landowner, the proper approach is to inquire first, whether any equitable estoppel exists, and if so, the extent of the equity established by the estoppel, before next considering how best that equity may be satisfied.

14     Whether there is an equity and its extent will, in the normal case, depend simply on the initial conduct said to give rise to the equity, although the court may have to decide how having regard to supervening circumstances, the equity can best be satisfied. If the equity so requires, the court may declare the defendant to be entitled to possession until his outlay is repaid, or even for so long as he wishes.

15     With regard to the creation of the estoppel, the general principle is that an estoppel arises where one party (A) makes a representation or promise to another party (B) intending (B) to act in reliance on the representation or promise, and (B) does so act to his detriment. (A) will then be estopped from acting inconsistently with his representation or promise.

16     In this particular case, the uncontrovertible facts show that:

(a)     prior to moving to the present premises (38A), the defendant and her family were staying at Lot 234 which belonged to the defendant’s elder sister, one Soh Thow Neo;

(b)     the house at 38A was built by the defendant’s late husband with the permission of the then owner of the land; and

the defendant and her family had been residing at 38A from 1956 or thereabouts.

17     However, the crucial fact in dispute was the nature of the representation or promise made to the defendant and her family which encouraged or induced them to move to 38A and expend money in building a house on land which did not belong to them and thereafter to stay on the premises.

18     On this point, the trial judge had made a finding of fact based on oral testimony, after having considered the evidence of the witnesses and formed his opinion on their credibility. We are unable to say that on the evidence before him the trial judge’s finding on this crucial fact was wrong, and we are of the opinion that that finding ought not, on well-established principles, to have been disturbed.

19     The finding of fact in question in this present case relates to a finding of a specific fact (ie whether the representation as alleged by the defendant had been made) and not to the drawing of an inference. A distinction must be drawn between a judge’s finding of a specific or primary fact based on conflicting testimony, and the inferences to be drawn from undisputed facts or from facts deposed to by witnesses and found by the court. While an appellate court might be more willing to form an independent opinion upon the proper inference to be drawn, it would always be extremely reluctant to reject a finding by a judge of a primary fact deposed to by witnesses especially when the finding is based on the credibility or bearing of a witness. As has been said by this court in Goh Leng Kang v Teng Swee Lin [1974–1976] SLR(R) 124 at [27]:

In the final analysis it was for the trial judge to balance the probabilities and to evaluate the weight of evidence on either side and it is not for this court to do so. …

20     We would therefore restore the trial judge’s finding that no expectation that the defendant be permitted to stay at 38A permanently had been created. This does not, however, mean that the plaintiff should be entitled to an order for possession without more. At the very least, and this the plaintiff does not dispute, the defendant having expended money in building her house on the premises with the then owner’s consent, there is an equitable estoppel or an estoppel by acquiescence operating in favour of the defendant, thereby creating in her favour an equity which has to be satisfied before the plaintiff can recover possession.

21     On the basis of the trial judge’s finding, this equity obviously cannot extend to protecting the defendant’s occupation of 38A permanently. Neither would it therefore require that the defendant should be compensated with the costs of finding alternative accommodation in order that the equity may be satisfied.

22     On the evidence, the extent of the defendant’s equity was merely to protect her from eviction without being compensated for the money expended in erecting the house. The defendant’s equity can therefore be satisfied if she were so compensated.

23     As it was common ground that the replacement cost of the house has been valued at $24,207, we therefore restored the trial judge’s order that the plaintiff be entitled to possession of the premises upon payment of $24,207 to the defendant. We further ordered that the defendant vacate the premises by 31 May 1987.
